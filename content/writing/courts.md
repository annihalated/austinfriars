## Introduction
The judicial system is critical. To settle disputes between private
persons, and to constrain the power of the state, an effective
judicial system is necessary. When courts are broken, they impose
inordinate costs on everyone else, and everything begins to break.

Nicholas Decker has a [nice summary](https://nicholasdecker.substack.com/p/whats-the-matter-with-india) of the ways in which things break. But the general problem goes something like this: 

- If the judicial system does not work, people have no effective way
  to resolve disputes through law. When a firm hires managers, and the
  manager does something illegal or fraudulent, the firm has no
  mechanism to resolve that dispute. Firms solve this problem by
  hiring family members who they can trust. This reduces the quality
  of management. When firms are badly managed, resources are
  misallocated, productivity goes down, and economic growth suffers.
  
- If the judicial system does not work, firms have no redress against
  the state. When the state takes an illegal action against a firm, it
  cannot go to court and quickly get the action overturned. The firm,
  therefore, does not receive the protection of the law. This problem
  is solved through cronyism and corruption. The firm maintains
  relationships with politicians and bureaucrats to ensure that
  favourable conditions are obtained.

The judicial system is broken in India. The Supreme Court has over
90,000 cases pending. Judicial productivity is abysmal. Cases can take
decades to resolve. There are unfilled judicial vacancies. Judicial independence exists, but disappears when it most matters. 

	
### Broken numbers
Oh, and the pendency numbers? I'm not sure they're right either.
[Manivannan, Raman &
Zaveri-Shah](https://blog.theleapjournal.org/2025/10/beyond-pendency-counting-cases-correctly.html#gsc.tab=0),
found recently that "the caseload at the Original Side of the Bombay
High Court [...] is being overcounted by 66%." Not only are the
pendency figures worrying, we can't even trust the numbers. Before the
despondency struck, they hit me with one of my favourite paragraphs
ever about measurement:

> Measures of the economy such as GDP, inflation, and employment rate,
> took decades to be built and continue to be challenged and improved,
> by researchers and policy-makers alike. Similar sound systems for
> the measurement of court metrics, of which caseload is only one
> part, need to be developed. Such systems are imperative for any
> meaningful discussion on court reform.

So this is the good news. There is finally a small but serious policy
community that is trying to make sense of the numbers on Indian
courts. This is a tiny number of people; they are concentrated in
places like Trustbridge, XKDR Forum, AGAMI, JALDI, DAKSH, and so on.
There are people in India who think: we are just beginning the process
of acquiring good numbers, and we will keep going, correcting
statistic after statistic after statistic. This is how we start a
journey towards the frontier of maturity, a frontier manned by
organisations like the US Federal Reserve and the NBER and the OLS and
the Bank of England. This is true in field after field in India.


### Objective
This post is something of an homage to the people doing the work of
moving slowly towards that frontier. It is a restatement of what we
have learnt in the last fifteen years or so. It is an account of *my*
process of learning about this field.

I hope it will be useful to people who are getting started with
understanding courts in India.

## Starting with doctrine

In university, the approach to the question of "slow courts" was to
focus on the procedure or the number of judges. There was also some
discussion of court vacations. But there was no attempt to
systematically study this question.

I was exposed to the idea that reporting statistics might be useful
when I interned at the [Supreme Court Observer](https://scobserver.in)
and read Sudhir Krishnaswamy on this question:

> For those familiar with the thinking and policy debates on legal and
> judicial system reform, what is striking is how unchanging and
> unimaginative the strategies proposed to 'reform' the system are.
> Indian policy makers have over the years increased the supply of
> judges, diverted and dispersed cases to Alternative Dispute
> Resolution forums, special courts and tribunals with little or no
> improvement of the overall health of the legal system. Despite the
> litany of failures, our political and bureaucratic discourse is
> unable to move beyond these 'tried and failed' strategies that are
> unsystematic or fragmented, unempirical and without a nuanced
> normative foundation. Moreover, there is no effort to develop any
> critical insight into the causes of failure.
